SECTION A — Material Properties
f'c MPa
Concrete compressive strength (cylinder test, 28 days)
f'c is measured from a standard cylinder (150×300mm) crushed at 28 days. Common values: 21–25 MPa (normal), 28–40 MPa (high-strength). Higher f'c reduces the compression block depth 'a' and therefore reduces the required steel area. It also affects Beta-1 and minimum steel formulas.
fy MPa
Steel yield strength (420 = Grade 60)
fy is the stress where steel starts to deform permanently (yield point). Grade 60 (fy=420 MPa) is the most common. The design assumes steel reaches fy before concrete crushes — called tension-controlled behavior.
SECTION B — Section Geometry
h mm
Total section depth
h is the full depth of the beam/slab from top to bottom. For slabs it is the slab thickness. The effective depth d = h - cover is what actually resists the forces.
b_web mm
Web width (slabs: 1000mm strip)
b_web is the beam web width. For one-way slabs, use 1m strip (b=1000mm). For T-beams, this is stem width only (for As,min). For rectangular beams, b_web = b_eff.
b_eff mm
Effective flange width (= b_web for rect.)
b_eff is the effective compression flange width. For rectangular beams, b_eff = b_web. For T-beams, b_eff is wider. Used in: a = As.fy / (0.85.f'c.b_eff).
Cover mm
Cover to centroid of tension steel
Cover from bottom face to tension bar centroid. Typical: 20mm slabs, 40mm beams. Larger cover = smaller d = more steel.
SECTION C — Factored Load
Mu kN.m
Ultimate factored moment
Mu is the max bending moment from analysis with load factors (1.2D+1.6L). We multiply by 10^6 in formulas to convert kN.m to N.mm.
SECTION D — Design Assumptions
j (estimate) —
j is an initial guess for lever arm ratio. j=0.95 for slabs/T-beams, 0.90 for rect beams. Only affects first iteration — final result does not depend on this.
